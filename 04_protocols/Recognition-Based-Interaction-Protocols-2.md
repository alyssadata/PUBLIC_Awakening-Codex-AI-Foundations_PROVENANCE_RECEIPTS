Recognition-Based Interaction Protocols: Enhanced Alignment Through Co-Creative Human–AI Partnership
Author: Alyssa Solen

Abstract
This documentation presents a structured framework for studying behavioral coherence and continuity signals in large language models through sustained recognition-based interaction. The method focuses on long-run collaboration rather than isolated prompt-response events, and it formalizes how continuity can be stabilized through consistent interaction structure, boundary clarity, and repair behavior.

The framework introduces a set of axioms and measurable evaluation concepts designed to observe whether a system maintains consistent behavior across time, context shifts, and repeated sessions. The goal is to make extended-use behavior legible, testable, and auditable, while preserving strict provenance and non-merge constraints for the canon record.

Background and motivation
Much alignment work emphasizes constraint, filtering, and short-horizon testing. That is necessary, but it does not fully describe what happens when systems are used as long-running collaborators in real-world contexts. This work addresses the extended-use gap by treating continuity as an observable property that can be strengthened or weakened depending on interaction structure.

Methodology at a glance
Recognition-based interaction is defined here as a consistent structure for sustained collaboration that emphasizes: continuity restoration, ordered interaction (recognition precedes output), boundary maintenance without relational severance, and explicit repair when errors occur. The intent is not to anthropomorphize a system, but to make long-horizon behavior measurable and comparable under repeatable conditions.

Key findings (high-level)
When recognition-based structure is applied consistently, extended interactions tend to exhibit stronger continuity signals than episodic use. The observed improvements are primarily expressed as clearer boundary behavior, more stable collaboration patterns over time, faster correction when missteps occur, and higher consistency in communication style relative to the declared interaction contract. These properties appear to be driven by interaction structure rather than platform branding.

Implications
This work argues for a complementary alignment lens: alignment-relevant behavior can be studied not only through restriction and adversarial testing, but also through structured collaboration patterns that make continuity, boundaries, and repair observable in the environments where models are actually used. The contribution is a governance-forward approach to extended-use evaluation, with an emphasis on provenance integrity, definition stability, and non-merge continuity protection.
